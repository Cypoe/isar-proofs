The Ontological Narrative

1. The Ontological Substrate

At the foundation of the stack is the Substrate, formally defined as the term algebra (ITerm in ISAR.lean) containing variables, operators (identity norm, constant konst, composition comp, and duplication dup), and term application. While these serve as constructors for the syntactic rewrite calculus, they are constructively derived at the tensor layer from the four geometric primitives: Identity (I), Rewrite (R), Adjacency (A), and State (S). The operators are characterized by their rewriting laws (e.g., identity \(\text{norm} \cdot x \to x\) and constant \(\text{konst} \cdot x \cdot y \to x\)\). In BasisCompleteness.lean, we prove that the standard distributive combinator \(S\) is constructively definable from the basis and satisfies its expected reduction behavior, demonstrating the algebraic completeness of the basis.

2. The Invariant Layer & Quotient

Directly above the syntax is the Invariant Layer (\(\Delta\)), which is a terminal object in the category of admissible computational substrates. The quotient specifically is a coinvariant—more precisely, the cofinal quotient under operational equivalence. Formally, this is the bisimulation quotient: two terms are identified if and only if they share the same infinite continuation behavior (\(t_1 \sim t_2\), i.e., no observation can distinguish them). In process algebra, this relation is called bisimilarity (Park/Milner), and the quotient space is the final coalgebra of the observable functor. The invariant layer \(\Delta\) is the greatest fixed point of this observable equivalence relation—making it coinductive rather than inductive, witnessed from above rather than constructed from below.

3. Semantic Views

Rather than treating different formalisms—such as the lambda calculus, term rewriting systems, stack-based virtual machine bytecode, or set theories—as competing foundations, they are modeled as Semantic Views (the Kernel structure in KernelCategory.lean). A semantic view consists of a target carrier type, a decoding mapping back to the calculus, and an equivalence relation representing observational equivalence. Category-theoretic terminality (morphism_uniqueness) proves that any structure-preserving morphism from a semantic view factors uniquely through the canonical quotient, unifying different systems under a single algebraic framework.

4. Historical Note: Ross's Plex Tract

The term Plex is a direct homage to computer science pioneer Douglas T. Ross and his seminal 1961 paper, "A Generalized Technique for Symbol Manipulation and Numerical Calculation" (the Plex Tract). Ross introduced the "plex" as a unified structural entity combining data elements and topology pointers. In our framework, we realize Ross's vision mathematically: the substrate represents a universal topology network where computation is the invariant projection of structured relationships, rather than an arbitrary sequence of syntactic commands.

5. Probability as Logic with Weights

Rather than sitting apart from logic, Probability Calculus is structured as a quantitative extension of it. Under this order-theoretic view, propositions form a partially ordered set (poset) of entailment and refinement. In the classical case, this poset is a distributive, orthocomplemented Boolean lattice (Level 1). Probability calculus assigns weights to regions of this lattice (Level 2), expressing Laplace's Equiprobability or general measures. By weakening distributivity or moving to orthomodular structures, we obtain Quantum Logic modeling incompatible events (Level 3), and finally Effect Algebras for unsharp or partial propositions (Level 4).

6. Bayesian Occam & Meta-Scoring

Within the zoo, each specimen acts as a candidate model \(M\). Bayesian Model Selection operates as a meta-system over the entire zoo, scoring each specimen by its marginal likelihood (or evidence) \(p(D \mid M) = \int p(D \mid \theta, M) p(\theta \mid M) d\theta\). Highly flexible models with many free parameters or wide prior ranges are penalized because they spread their prior probability mass over parameter settings that do not fit the observed data. The relative Occam score represents the model's description length, mathematically proving why the minimal ISAR substrate is favored.

Verified Partial Evaluation & The Futamura Chain

The Mechanics of Futamura Projections

In Futamura.lean, we formally define substitution (subst) and partial evaluation (specialize) over variables and terms, and prove the correctness of the three Futamura projections:

1. First Futamura Projection (Specialization Soundness):

Given a program \(t\), a static environment mapping static inputs, and a dynamic environment mapping dynamic inputs, specialization produces a new program. We prove (futamura_first) that running the specialized program with the dynamic inputs yields the same result as running the original program with the complete environment: $$\text{subst}(\text{specialize}(t, env_{\text{static}}), env_{\text{dynamic}}) = \text{subst}(t, env)$$

2. Second Futamura Projection (Compiler Generation):

If we specialize a correct partial evaluator with respect to an interpreter, we generate a compiler. We prove (futamura_second) that applying this compiler to a program source yields a specialized executable program that behaves identically to the interpreter running that program: $$\text{specialize}(\text{specializer}, \text{interpreter}) = \text{compiler}$$

3. Third Futamura Projection (Compiler Generator):

If we specialize the partial evaluator with respect to itself, we generate a compiler generator (often called cogen). We prove (futamura_third) that applying cogen to any interpreter yields a compiler for that interpreter: $$\text{specialize}(\text{specializer}, \text{specializer}) = \text{cogen}$$

ISAR vs. Classical Systems

How does ISAR compare to classical "code = data" systems? We define five meta-properties to highlight the unique categorical and structural features verified in the ISAR vertical stack.

The Structural Question

"What additional structural properties does ISAR possess compared to classical 'code = data' systems like Lisp, Lambda, Smalltalk, etc.?"

1. The Standard Core (IK-Fragment)

In the IK-fragment (without Adjacency and Rewrite operators), ISAR operates on the same level as Lisp or untyped Lambda calculus. Code and data are homogeneous, SKI is embedded, and confluence guarantees Turing-completeness.

2. The Richer Substrate (Full ISAR)

Full ISAR introduces first-class representation of **Identity (I)**, **Rewrite (R)**, **Adjacency (A)**, and **State (S)**. It constructs a stable operational quotient (the bisimulation final coalgebra) and proves categorical terminality, making it a universal dialect mediator.

The Epistemological Paradox: CIC vs. ISAR

To verify the universal substrate, we employ Lean 4, a proof assistant based on the Calculus of Inductive Constructions (CIC). This introduces a subtle paradox: we use a typed, terminating metalanguage (Lean) to prove properties of a Turing-complete, potentially diverging object language (ISAR). We resolve this by decoupling execution from reasoning. Lean does not run infinite computations; it reasons statically over inductive step relations, allowing terminating logic to certify general-recursive dynamics.

The Gauge Shadow: Category-theoretic terminality demands that any two matrix models (\(K_1\), \(K_2\)) of the substrate must be isomorphic. At the carrier matrix layer, this algebraic isomorphism manifests precisely as a gauge similarity transformation: \(P \cdot K_1 \cdot P^{-1} = K_2\). The gauge-dependence represents our choice of coordinates (the matrix entries), while the gauge-invariance represents the terminal semantics.
